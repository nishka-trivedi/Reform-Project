Background & Biography

Who Was Lucy Stone?

Lucy Stone grew up on a farm in rural Massachusetts, the eighth of nine children. From an early age she saw how differently her mother was treated compared to her father. Her mother worked just as hard on the farm but had no legal control over the money it earned, no say over property, and no guaranteed right to her own children. Stone found this deeply unfair, and it stayed with her.

"I was a woman before I was an abolitionist. I must speak for the women."

She spent nine years teaching school to save enough money for college, earning far less than male teachers doing the same job. At 25 she enrolled at Oberlin College, which was one of the only schools in the country that admitted women. But even there she ran into obstacles. When she was selected to write a commencement essay, she learned that a male professor would have to read it aloud on her behalf, since women weren't allowed to speak at graduation. She refused to write it at all. It was one of her first public acts of protest.

After graduating in 1847, she began lecturing on abolition and women's rights, quickly becoming one of the most well-known speakers in the country. Crowds sometimes threw things at her or poured cold water through windows while she spoke. She kept going anyway.

In 1855 she married Henry Blackwell, an abolitionist who shared her values. Their wedding ceremony included a written protest against marriage laws that took away women's legal rights, and she kept her own last name, which was almost unheard of at the time. Women who did the same in later years were sometimes called "Lucy Stoners" in her honor.

✦

In 1869 she co-founded the American Woman Suffrage Association, and in 1870 she launched The Woman's Journal, a weekly newspaper focused on women's rights that she edited for over twenty years. It became the most widely read suffrage publication in the country. She kept working until she died in 1893, never getting to see women win the right to vote. The 19th Amendment passed 27 years after her death.

Primary Source

Marriage Protest of Lucy Stone and Henry B. Blackwell
Read Aloud at Their Wedding Ceremony · May 1, 1855

While acknowledging our mutual affection by publicly assuming the relationship of husband and wife, yet in justice to ourselves and a great principle, we deem it a duty to declare that this act on our part implies no sanction of, nor promise of voluntary obedience to such of the present laws of marriage, as refuse to recognize the wife as an independent, rational being...

...while they confer upon the husband an injurious and unnatural superiority, investing him with legal powers which no honorable man would exercise, and which no man should possess.

📌 Historian's Note: Stone and Blackwell are targeting "coverture," the doctrine that merged a woman's identity into her husband's upon marriage. They also argue against a power that corrupts men who hold it, which was a smart way to appeal to male readers at the time.

We believe that personal independence and equal human rights can never be forfeited, except for crime; that marriage should be an equal and permanent partnership...

...and so recognized by law; that until it is so recognized, married partners should provide against the radical injustice of present laws, by every means in their power.

📌 Historian's Note: "By every means in their power" is deliberately open-ended. It signals that Stone saw civil disobedience, like her later tax protest, as a moral obligation rather than just a political strategy.

Thus reverencing law, we enter our protest against rules and customs which are unworthy of the name, since they violate justice, the essence of law.

📌 Historian's Note: Stone and Blackwell argue they are the true defenders of law by opposing laws that violate justice. This framing, where protesters claim to uphold higher principles than the laws they break, would be used by civil rights movements for the next 150 years.

Analyze the Document

Decoding the Protest

This document was read aloud by Henry Blackwell at their wedding and reprinted in newspapers across the country shortly after. It was a deliberate public statement, not just a private vow. Click each question to look more closely at what it says and why it mattered.

Why publish a wedding protest in a newspaper?

Stone knew that press coverage would reach far more people than any single speech. By making their protest public, she turned a private ceremony into a conversation the whole country could have. She wasn't just objecting to her own situation; she was showing other people what a more equal marriage could look like.

Why did keeping her surname matter legally?

Under the laws of the time, when a woman married, her legal identity was essentially absorbed into her husband's. She couldn't own property in her own name, sign her own contracts, or keep her own earnings. Keeping her name was Stone's way of refusing to accept that arrangement, even symbolically. It forced a small legal confrontation every time she signed a document or introduced herself, which kept the issue visible.

Who was she trying to reach?

Several different audiences at once: reformers who would feel energized, everyday readers who might not have thought about marriage law before, lawmakers who needed to see that people were paying attention, and women in difficult marriages who had no legal way out. Stone was naming their situation as an injustice rather than just an unfortunate fact of life.

How does this connect to her work against slavery?

Stone saw a clear parallel between slavery and what marriage law did to women. Both systems gave one person legal control over another. The language she and Blackwell use in the protest, about personal independence and rights that cannot be taken away, was the same language abolitionists used. She was drawing that connection on purpose.

Obstacles Faced

The Walls She Broke Through

Legal Invisibility

Under the laws of the time, a married woman had no real legal standing. She couldn't own property, sign contracts, keep her own wages, or be guaranteed custody of her children. Stone was trying to change a system that didn't even recognize her as a full legal person.

Hostility and Physical Intimidation

Stone faced real danger when she lectured. Crowds heckled her, threw things, and sometimes poured cold water through windows while she spoke. Newspapers called her "unwomanly." Churches refused to let her use their halls. She showed up anyway.

A Divided Movement

After the Civil War, the women's rights movement split badly over the 15th Amendment. Stone believed supporting Black male suffrage was the right thing to do morally, even though it meant women still couldn't vote. Other leaders, including Stanton and Anthony, opposed the amendment and sometimes made explicitly racist arguments. Stone was trying to hold onto her principles while keeping a coalition together, which wasn't always possible.

Closed Doors Everywhere

Even at Oberlin, which was considered progressive, women were barred from speaking publicly and expected to do domestic work for male students. Law, medicine, the clergy, and politics were almost entirely closed to women. Stone had to fight for basic access at every institution she encountered.

Action Plan & Strategies

How She Fought

Public Speaking on the Lecture Circuit

Before radio, television, or the internet, the lyceum lecture circuit was how ideas spread. Traveling speakers would go town to town, drawing large audiences who came to be informed and entertained. Stone became one of the most popular lecturers in the country.

She charged admission for her talks, which was practical on two levels: it paid for her work, and it showed that people were willing to pay to hear a woman speak. Her speeches were regularly reported in newspapers, which extended her reach well beyond whoever was in the room.

One thing she refused to do was keep women's rights separate from abolition. The Anti-Slavery Society sometimes tried to limit what she could talk about, but she insisted on raising both issues. She believed the two causes were connected and said so publicly.

Who she was trying to reach: Ordinary Americans, including voters and lawmakers, and women who had never been told that what they were experiencing was unjust rather than just normal.

By the Numbers
Stone lectured across nearly every Northern state, often delivering multiple speeches per week for years at a stretch.
She earned enough from lecturing to fund her activism independently, which was unusual financial autonomy for any woman of the era.
A single Stone lecture could be reported in a dozen newspapers, multiplying her audience far beyond the room.

The Woman's Journal: A Newspaper Built to Last

Stone understood that speeches faded from memory but a published newspaper stuck around. In 1870 she co-founded The Woman's Journal, a weekly paper dedicated to women's rights. It ran for 47 years, long past her death, and became the most widely read suffrage publication in the country.

The paper covered legislative news, printed women's own accounts of their experiences, and kept the issue of suffrage in front of readers week after week. It was a way of sustaining public attention during the long stretches when nothing dramatic was happening.

Stone also understood how to get press coverage for her other actions. Her wedding protest was sent directly to major newspapers. Her tax refusal in 1858 was staged publicly so reporters would be there to write about it. She wasn't just doing things; she was making sure people heard about them.

Key Facts
The Woman's Journal ran from 1870 to 1917, 47 years in total, outlasting Stone by 24 years.
Stone's daughter Alice continued editing it after her mother's death, keeping it going through to ratification of the 19th Amendment.
The paper was later absorbed into The Woman Citizen, which continued into the 1920s.

Using Protest to Make Injustice Visible

One of Stone's most effective tactics was doing something publicly that forced people to confront an unfair law. In 1858 she refused to pay property taxes on her home in New Jersey. Her argument was straightforward: women who couldn't vote had no say in how their tax money was spent, which violated the same principle Americans used to justify the Revolution.

Tax collectors came and took her furniture, her dishes, and her baby daughter's cradle. Stone made sure journalists were there to see it. The image of a mother's belongings being hauled away over a principled stand got picked up by newspapers across the country.

Her wedding ceremony worked the same way. It wasn't a private event; it was a public statement with a formal written protest, designed to be published. She wasn't just objecting to unfair laws, she was making those laws impossible to ignore.

The Tax Resistance
Stone's 1858 tax protest predated the organized tax resistance of the British suffragettes by nearly 50 years.
"No taxation without representation" resonated strongly because it was the founding principle of the American Revolution, now being applied to half the population.

Building Organizations That Would Outlast Her

Stone recognized early on that individual speeches and protests mattered, but permanent change required permanent organizations. She helped put together some of the first national women's rights conventions, co-founded the American Woman Suffrage Association in 1869, and spent decades on the slower, less visible work of building infrastructure.

She believed the best path to a federal amendment was winning state by state first. States that granted women the right to vote would prove the idea worked and create political pressure for national change. Wyoming, Colorado, Utah, and Idaho all granted women's suffrage while Stone was alive, in part because of this sustained organizing.

She also kept up working relationships with Black civil rights leaders and Republican politicians, even when it cost her politically. She thought those alliances mattered and refused to abandon them for short-term gains.

Alliances Built
Stone maintained close ties with Frederick Douglass, who spoke at women's rights conventions and saw both causes as connected.
Her partnership with Julia Ward Howe helped bring prominent cultural figures into the suffrage movement, giving it broader social legitimacy.
The AWSA's state-focused strategy laid important groundwork for the 19th Amendment's eventual passage.

Stone died in 1893, 27 years before women got the right to vote. But the organizations she built, the newspaper she ran, and the network she spent decades maintaining were all still active when the 19th Amendment passed in 1920. She didn't see the finish line, but she helped build the road to it.

Her state-by-state approach turned out to be the right one. States that had already granted women's suffrage showed that it worked and created political pressure for a national amendment. The Woman's Journal kept running after her death, edited by her daughter Alice, and continued to be a major voice for suffrage until it was no longer needed.

Keeping her own last name after marriage was a smaller but lasting act. It sparked legal arguments and social debates that continued for over a hundred years. Some states still required married women to take their husband's names as late as the 1970s. The Lucy Stone League, founded in 1921, kept fighting that specific battle in her name. Her work also directly influenced later organizers who used the same state-by-state strategy, including advocates for marriage equality in the early 2000s, showing that her approach to building change has stayed relevant long after her death.
